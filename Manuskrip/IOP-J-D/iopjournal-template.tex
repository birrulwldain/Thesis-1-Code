\begin{abstract}
Laser-Induced Breakdown Spectroscopy (LIBS) enables rapid multi-element analysis without extensive sample preparation, yet quantifying elemental concentrations in complex geological matrices remains challenging due to spectral overlap, matrix effects, and plasma variability. Existing machine learning models for LIBS treat the measured spectrum as a single composite signal and thus disregard the physically meaningful decomposition into individual elemental (monoatomic) contributions. This work proposes a CNN--Transformer encoder--decoder architecture that explicitly models the relationship between monoatomic component spectra and the observed polyatomic mixture spectrum. The encoder, fed with individual monoatomic spectra produced by a Saha--Boltzmann forward model, is designed to learn element-specific latent representations through self-attention. The decoder receives the polyatomic mixture spectrum and recovers elemental concentrations via cross-attention over the encoder output, effectively learning the compositional rules that govern spectral superposition. One-dimensional convolutional layers precede both encoder and decoder to extract local emission-line features before the Transformer captures long-range wavelength dependencies. The model will be pre-trained on synthetic spectra generated from NIST Atomic Spectra Database transitions and fine-tuned on experimental LIBS measurements of volcanic soil samples from Aceh, Indonesia, using X-ray fluorescence (XRF) data as ground-truth concentrations. Baseline comparisons against CNN-only, Transformer-only, partial least-squares, and Informer models are planned. This paper presents the proposed methodology; experimental validation and results will be reported upon completion of model training.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================================================

% --- General Background ---
\textbf{Laser-Induced Breakdown Spectroscopy (LIBS) is an emission spectroscopy technique that has been widely applied to elemental analysis of complex materials, including minerals, rocks, and soils \cite{legnaioli_laser-induced_2025}.}Its ability to perform rapid multi-element analysis with minimal sample preparation makes it attractive for geochemical prospecting and natural-resource exploration \cite{sawyers_database_2025}. Multiple studies have demonstrated successful elemental quantification in geological samples through approaches ranging from multivariate regression and univariate calibration curves to machine-learning methods \cite{babos_laser-induced_2024, manzoor_machine_2025, zhang_improving_2025}. Although these approaches can achieve high analytical accuracy, their performance depends strongly on the spectral modelling strategy and on the sensitivity of the method to matrix effects in compositionally heterogeneous samples \cite{hao_machine_2024, manelski_libs_2024}. This dependence is particularly pronounced in volcanic soils, which inherently exhibit complex multi-element compositions with large matrix variability. Among quantitative LIBS approaches, Calibration-Free LIBS (CF-LIBS) enables elemental quantification without reference standards by extracting fundamental plasma parameters, namely the electron temperature $T_e$ and the electron density $n_e$, under the assumption of Local Thermodynamic Equilibrium (LTE) \cite{cristoforetti_local_2010}. However, the assumption of a homogeneous LTE plasma is often inadequate for describing the highly transient, spatially graded plasmas generated by nanosecond laser pulses \cite{zaitsev_two-zone_2024, bultel_mcwhirter_2025}. Deviations from LTE induce spectral distortions through optical opacity and self-absorption, which destabilise temperature and density estimates \cite{tang_review_2024, hansen_modeling_2021}.

% --- Previous Findings ---
Physics-based approaches such as the MERLIN adaptive radiative-transfer model have addressed these distortions by coupling Saha--Boltzmann population distributions with the radiative transfer equation, thereby reproducing emission spectra with thermodynamic consistency \cite{favre_merlin_2025, favre_towards_2025}. Despite their physical rigour, these forward models entail computationally expensive nonlinear inversions that limit their applicability for routine analysis. Machine learning and deep learning have emerged as alternatives that learn the nonlinear mapping from spectra to plasma parameters directly from data \cite{favre_towards_2025, liu_remote_2026, wang_simulation_2024}. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) capture local spectral features such as emission-line profiles, while Transformer architectures model long-range dependencies across the wavelength axis \cite{vaswani_attention_2017, liu_remote_2026}. Hybrid CNN--Transformer models in particular have demonstrated promising results for quantifying trace elements in steel \cite{liu_remote_2026} and for classifying spectral signatures in multi-element samples \cite{walidain_informer-based_2026}. Nevertheless, most data-driven models operate as black boxes that do not explicitly encode the physics governing spectrum formation \cite{hao_machine_2024}. This lack of physical structure limits generalisation and demands large experimental datasets for reliable training. A fundamental physical fact, however, remains unexploited in current modelling practice: a polyatomic LIBS spectrum is, to a first approximation, a concentration-weighted superposition of monoatomic component spectra. All existing models, whether physics-based or data-driven, treat the measured spectrum as a single signal and thereby ignore this compositional structure \cite{liu_remote_2026, favre_towards_2025}. \textbf{No prior work has explicitly modelled the decomposition of a mixture spectrum into its monoatomic constituents within a learning framework. Cross-attention, a mechanism originally proposed for sequence-to-sequence translation \cite{vaswani_attention_2017}, is ideally suited for this task: the decoder can query encoder-produced element representations to discover which monoatomic contributions compose a given polyatomic observation.}

% --- State of the Art ---
\textbf{This paper presents a CNN--Transformer encoder--decoder architecture that introduces explicit mono--polyatomic spectral decomposition into quantitative LIBS analysis. The encoder processes individual monoatomic spectra generated by a Saha--Boltzmann forward model to learn element-specific latent representations.} The decoder receives the polyatomic mixture spectrum and predicts elemental concentrations through cross-attention over the encoder output. One-dimensional convolutional layers extract local emission-line features before the Transformer stages capture global wavelength dependencies. The model is pre-trained on synthetic spectra derived from the NIST Atomic Spectra Database and fine-tuned on experimental LIBS measurements of volcanic soil from Aceh, Indonesia, with X-ray fluorescence (XRF) analysis providing ground-truth concentrations. Contributions of this work are threefold: (i) an encoder--decoder framework that structurally separates monoatomic from polyatomic spectral representations for LIBS, (ii) a cross-attention mechanism that enables physically interpretable concentration regression, and (iii) application to complex volcanic-soil matrices from Aceh, Indonesia.

\subsection{Framework overview}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The proposed approach followed a two-stage workflow.
In the first stage \emph{(offline)}, a physics-based forward model generated pairs of monoatomic and polyatomic synthetic spectra across a grid of plasma conditions; these served as training data.
In the second stage \emph{(online)}, a CNN--Transformer encoder--decoder consumed the spectral pairs and predicted elemental concentrations.
This separation allowed the forward model to serve purely as a data generator, while the deep-learning architecture was responsible for learning the inverse mapping from spectra to concentrations.

\subsection{Synthetic data generation} \label{sec:synth}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Synthetic spectra were produced under the assumption of LTE, which is reasonable for the early-to-mid temporal window of nanosecond LIBS plasmas \cite{cristoforetti_local_2010}.
The population of each ionisation stage is governed by the Saha equation \cite{fujimoto_plasma_2004},
\begin{equation} \label{eq:saha}
\frac{n_e \, n_{z+1}}{n_z} = 2\,\frac{Z_{z+1}(T_e)}{Z_z(T_e)} \left(\frac{2\pi m_e k_B T_e}{h^2}\right)^{3/2} \exp\!\left(-\frac{E_{\mathrm{ion},z}}{k_B T_e}\right),
\end{equation}
where $n_z$ and $n_{z+1}$ are the number densities of ionisation stages $z$ and $z+1$, $Z_z$ denotes the partition function, $E_{\mathrm{ion},z}$ is the ionisation energy, and the remaining symbols have their usual meanings.
Within each ionisation stage the excited-state populations follow the Boltzmann distribution \cite{fujimoto_plasma_2004},
\begin{equation} \label{eq:boltz}
n_i = \frac{n_z \, g_i \exp(-E_i / k_B T_e)}{Z_z(T_e)},
\end{equation}
where $g_i$ and $E_i$ are the degeneracy and energy of level $i$.

The spectral emission coefficient for a transition $k \to i$ is \cite{fujimoto_plasma_2004}
\begin{equation} \label{eq:emiss}
j_\lambda = \frac{hc}{4\pi\lambda}\,A_{ki}\,n_k\,\phi_V(\lambda),
\end{equation}
with Einstein coefficient $A_{ki}$ and Voigt profile $\phi_V(\lambda)$.
The Voigt profile arises from the convolution of a Gaussian component due to Doppler broadening \cite{griem_spectral_1974},
\begin{equation} \label{eq:doppler}
\Delta\lambda_D = \lambda_{ki}\sqrt{\frac{8\ln 2\,k_B T_i}{m_k c^2}},
\end{equation}
and a Lorentzian component dominated by Stark broadening \cite{griem_spectral_1974},
\begin{equation} \label{eq:stark}
\Delta\lambda_S \approx 2\,\omega_{ki}(T_e)\,\frac{n_e}{n_{e,\mathrm{ref}}},
\end{equation}
where $\omega_{ki}$ is the electron-impact half-width at reference density $n_{e,\mathrm{ref}}$.

Atomic transition data ($\lambda_{ki}$, $A_{ki}$, $g_i$, $E_i$) were taken from the NIST Atomic Spectra Database \cite{kramida_nist_2024}.
The spectral window covered 200--900~nm with a resolution of $\Delta\lambda = 0.02$~nm, yielding approximately 35\,000 spectral channels per spectrum.
Seven major elements were simulated: Si, Al, Fe, Ca, Mg, Na, and K, whose concentration ranges were chosen to reflect the geochemistry of volcanic soils (table~\ref{tab:comp}).
Plasma parameters were uniformly randomly sampled within $T_e \in [6\,000,\;15\,000]$~K and $n_e \in [10^{16},\;10^{17}]$~cm$^{-3}$, yielding a total of 10\,000 synthetic spectra.
For each plasma condition and each element $z$, a monoatomic spectrum $S_{\mathrm{mono}}^{(z)}(\lambda)$ was computed by summing equation~(\ref{eq:emiss}) over all transitions of that element.
The corresponding polyatomic spectrum was constructed as a concentration-weighted superposition,
\begin{equation} \label{eq:poly}
S_{\mathrm{poly}}(\lambda) = \sum_{z} c_z \, S_{\mathrm{mono}}^{(z)}(\lambda),
\end{equation}
where $c_z$ is the fractional concentration of element $z$, with $\sum_z c_z = 1$.
Finally, each synthetic spectrum was convolved with an instrumental Gaussian of full width at half maximum $\Delta\lambda_{\mathrm{inst}} = 0.02$~nm to match the spectral resolution of the echelle spectrometer.
The resulting dataset was split into 80\% training and 20\% test partitions.

\subsection{Experimental data} \label{sec:exp}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Experimental LIBS spectra were acquired from 24~volcanic-soil samples collected on the flanks of Gunung Seulawah Agam, Aceh, Sumatra, Indonesia.
Samples were taken from four cardinal directions (North, West, South, East) at three depths (0--20, 20--40, and 40--60~cm), yielding a spatially representative dataset of the volcanic edifice.
Each soil pellet was ablated with a Q-switched Nd:YAG laser operating at 1064~nm with a pulse energy of 114~mJ.
Plasma emission was collected and dispersed by an echelle spectrometer coupled to an optical multichannel analyser (OMA) detector.
Three laser shots were acquired per sample and averaged to improve the signal-to-noise ratio.

Ground-truth elemental concentrations were obtained independently by X-ray fluorescence (XRF) analysis.
The XRF concentrations served as regression targets during fine-tuning, while CF-LIBS derived concentrations \cite{cristoforetti_local_2010} provided supplementary validation.

\subsection{CNN--Transformer encoder--decoder architecture} \label{sec:model}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The architecture comprised four modules: (i) a shared 1D~CNN feature extractor, (ii) a Transformer encoder operating on monoatomic representations, (iii) a Transformer decoder with cross-attention operating on the polyatomic spectrum, and (iv) a regression head that outputs elemental concentrations.
Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} shows the complete model.
The hyperparameters used throughout the experiments are listed in table~\ref{tab:hyper}.

The decoder (right) processes polyatomic spectra through masked self-attention and cross-attention with the encoder output. \subsubsection{Input representation.}

A monoatomic spectrum $S_{\mathrm{mono}}^{(z)}(\lambda) \in \mathbb{R}^{L}$ is a one-dimensional signal of length $L$ (number of wavelength channels) corresponding to element~$z$.
The polyatomic spectrum $S_{\mathrm{poly}}(\lambda) \in \mathbb{R}^{L}$ is the experimentally measured or synthetically generated mixture signal.
Both were normalised to unit maximum intensity before being input to the model.

\subsubsection{1D CNN feature extractor.}

Each spectrum was passed through a stack of three 1D convolutional layers \cite{he_deep_2016} with kernel sizes of 7, 5, and 3 and filter counts of 64, 128, and 128, respectively, to capture local emission-line patterns:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F} = \mathrm{ReLU}\!\bigl(\mathrm{BN}(\mathrm{Conv1D}(S;\,\Theta_{\mathrm{cnn}}))\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{BN}$ denotes batch normalisation \cite{ioffe_batch_2015} and $\Theta_{\mathrm{cnn}}$ are the learnable convolutional parameters.
The CNN parameters were shared between the encoder and decoder paths to enforce a common spectral feature space.
The output is a sequence of feature vectors $\mathbf{F} \in \mathbb{R}^{L' \times d}$, where $L'$ is the reduced sequence length after pooling and $d = 128$ is the feature dimension.

\subsubsection{Transformer encoder.}

The encoder received the CNN features of all $Z$ monoatomic spectra, concatenated along the sequence dimension, together with learnable element-type and positional embeddings \cite{vaswani_attention_2017}.
Multi-head self-attention with $h = 8$ heads learns intra-element and inter-element dependencies:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Attention}(\mathbf{Q},\mathbf{K},\mathbf{V}) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\mathbf{V}.
\end{equation}
After $N_{\mathrm{enc}} = 4$ encoder layers, the output is a set of element-specific latent representations $\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{enc}} \in \mathbb{R}^{(Z \cdot L') \times d}$.

\subsubsection{Transformer decoder with cross-attention.}

The decoder operated on the CNN features of the polyatomic spectrum \cite{vaswani_attention_2017}.
Each decoder layer contained three sub-layers: masked self-attention over the mixture features, cross-attention with the encoder output, and a position-wise feed-forward network.
The cross-attention mechanism is the key structural innovation:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Q}_{\mathrm{dec}} = \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{poly}}\,\mathbf{W}_Q, \quad
\mathbf{K}_{\mathrm{enc}} = \mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{enc}}\,\mathbf{W}_K, \quad
\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{enc}} = \mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{enc}}\,\mathbf{W}_V,
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{poly}}$ denotes the decoder's polyatomic features and $\mathbf{W}_Q, \mathbf{W}_K, \mathbf{W}_V$ are learnable projections.
The resulting cross-attention scores have a physically meaningful interpretation: high attention weight between a decoder position (a spectral region of the mixture) and an encoder position (an element-specific feature) indicates that the corresponding element contributes to the mixture intensity at that wavelength.

After $N_{\mathrm{dec}} = 4$ decoder layers, a global average pooling and a linear projection yielded the predicted concentration vector:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{c}} = \mathrm{Linear}\!\bigl(\mathrm{GAP}(\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{dec}})\bigr) \in \mathbb{R}^{Z}.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Training strategy.}

The model was trained in two phases.

\emph{Pre-training on synthetic data.}
The encoder--decoder was trained on $(\{S_{\mathrm{mono}}^{(z)}\}_{z=1}^{Z},\,S_{\mathrm{poly}},\,\mathbf{c})$ triplets generated by the forward model (section~\ref{sec:synth}).
The loss function combined a concentration regression term and a spectral reconstruction term:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:loss}
\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{Z}\sum_{z=1}^{Z}(\hat{c}_z - c_z)^2 + \alpha\,\|S_{\mathrm{poly}} - \hat{S}_{\mathrm{poly}}\|_2^2,
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 0.1$ is the reconstruction regularisation weight.
The Adam optimiser \cite{kingma_adam_2015} was used with an initial learning rate of $10^{-4}$ and a cosine annealing schedule \cite{loshchilov_sgdr_2017}.

\emph{Fine-tuning on experimental data.}
The pre-trained model was fine-tuned on experimental LIBS spectra with XRF-derived concentrations as targets.
During fine-tuning the monoatomic spectra were generated by the forward model at plasma conditions estimated from each experimental spectrum.
The learning rate was reduced to $10^{-5}$ to preserve the representations learned during pre-training.

\emph{Domain adaptation.}
To further bridge the synthetic--experimental domain gap, a domain-adversarial training strategy \cite{ganin_domain_2016} was employed.
A Gradient Reversal Layer (GRL) was attached to the shared Transformer encoder, feeding into a domain classifier that distinguished synthetic from experimental spectra.
During backpropagation the GRL reversed the gradient sign, encouraging the encoder to learn domain-invariant representations.
Figure~\ref{fig:domain} illustrates the domain-adaptation architecture.

\subsection{Evaluation protocol} \label{sec:eval}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Model performance was quantified by three metrics computed per element:

\begin{itemize}
\item Root Mean Square Error: $\mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(c_i - \hat{c}_i)^2}$
\item Coefficient of determination: $R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum(c_i - \hat{c}_i)^2}{\sum(c_i - \bar{c})^2}$
\item Mean Absolute Percentage Error: $\mathrm{MAPE} = \frac{100\%}{n}\sum\left|\frac{c_i - \hat{c}_i}{c_i}\right|$
\end{itemize}

Four baseline models were compared:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{PLS}: Partial least-squares regression \cite{wold_pls_2001} on the raw spectrum.
\item \textbf{CNN-only}: The shared CNN feature extractor followed by a linear regression head, without Transformer layers.
\item \textbf{Transformer-only}: A Transformer encoder applied directly to the raw polyatomic spectrum without CNN preprocessing or mono--polyatomic decomposition.
\item \textbf{Informer}: The ProbSparse self-attention Transformer of \cite{zhou_informer_2021}, adapted for spectral regression following \cite{walidain_informer-based_2026}.
\end{enumerate}

All models were evaluated using stratified $k$-fold cross-validation ($k = 5$) on the experimental dataset.
Additionally, a domain-transfer experiment assessed generalisation from synthetic to experimental data without fine-tuning.

It should be noted that several sources of uncertainty affect the reported metrics.
The XRF ground-truth concentrations carry an inherent measurement uncertainty of approximately 1--5\% relative, which propagates into the regression targets.
Furthermore, the LTE assumption underlying the synthetic data may introduce a systematic bias when the model is applied to experimental spectra acquired outside the early-time plasma window.
These limitations are discussed further in the concluding section.
