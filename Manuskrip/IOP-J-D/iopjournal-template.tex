\title{Physics-Informed Surrogate Model Based on Partial-LTE and Two-Zone Radiative Transfer Equation for Calibration-Free Plasma Diagnostics}

\begin{abstract}
Quantitative analysis in Calibration-Free Laser-Induced Breakdown Spectroscopy (CF-LIBS) heavily relies on the assumption of a homogeneous plasma in local thermodynamic equilibrium (LTE).
% However, transient plasmas possess severe spatial gradients and optical opacities, triggering self-absorption that distorts the emission spectrum. Furthermore, pure collisional-radiative (CR) kinetic models often fail due to the absence of complete collisional cross-section data in standard databases (the "Atomic Data Desert" phenomenon). To overcome these limitations, this study proposes a deterministic two-zone (Core-Shell) forward model that couples partial-LTE (pLTE) with the Radiative Transfer Equation (RTE). The pLTE paradigm stabilizes the internal excitation population from the deficit of collisional data, while the RTE analytically resolves the macroscopic self-reversal effect. By integrating exact Stark broadening parameters from literature rather than using probabilistic correlations, the model accurately reproduces Fraunhofer-like spectral depressions on optically thick resonance transitions. This deterministic architecture completely bypasses black-box machine learning algorithms on the fundamental physics engine, providing a physically rigorous calibration-free synthetic spectrum generator. This framework significantly reduces quantitative uncertainties in analyzing complex matrix materials, offering a robust foundation for dynamic plasma diagnostics.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The assumption of a homogeneous plasma in pure Local Thermodynamic Equilibrium (LTE) acts as a fundamental prerequisite for quantitative analysis methods in calibration-free Laser-Induced Breakdown Spectroscopy (CF-LIBS) \cite{favre_towards_2025, cristoforetti_local_2010}. However, this theoretical prerequisite has been proven to fail in describing the recombination dynamics of real plasmas with severe gradients \cite{favre_merlin_2025, zaitsev_two-zone_2024}. Under ideal conditions, this thermodynamic framework ensures that the fundamental plasma parameters, namely electron temperature ($T_e$) and electron density ($n_e$), can be extracted precisely to directly quantify elemental composition, which is also a primary foundation in modeling ionic mixtures in astrophysical plasmas \cite{fritzsche_atomic_2025}. Conversely, deviations from the LTE condition not only distort analytical accuracy but also render the quantification mechanism invalid \cite{cristoforetti_local_2010, hansen_modeling_2021, zaytsev_accurate_2026}. Given this complexity, formulating a rigorously validated plasma characterization becomes essential, particularly in the analysis of complex matrix materials that are inherently susceptible to spatial inhomogeneity anomalies during observation, such as those applied in space exploration instruments \cite{sawyers_database_2025, manelski_libs_2024}, analysis of rocks under extreme atmospheric conditions \cite{zhang_improving_2025}, in-situ characterization of liquid metal phase transitions \cite{leosson_analysis_2022}, evaluation of spacecraft heat shield abrasion during atmospheric reentry \cite{leistikow_quantification_2026}, and in-situ monitoring of nuclear fusion reactor walls \cite{marin_roldan_libs_2021}.

Conventional efforts to justify such absolute equilibrium traditionally rely on the McWhirter criterion (1965). However, this classical formulation has been empirically proven to be a necessary but insufficient condition to describe real plasma dynamics \cite{bultel_mcwhirter_2025}. This validation failure roots in two destructive distortions. Spatially, thermal gradients trigger a self-absorption effect that disrupts the linearity of the emission spectral profile \cite{tang_review_2024}. Thermodynamically, anomalies in molecular energy level populations such as the intensity of vibration-rotation transitions exhibit extreme deviations from conventional LTE predictions thus demanding a multi-temperature approach \cite{zaytsev_accurate_2026}. To mitigate this spatial anomaly, several recent computational studies have begun integrating the Artificial Neural Network (ANN) architecture with a two-zone model \cite{zaitsev_two-zone_2024, amogh_libs_2026,m_s_machine_2025}. Although this data-driven computational approach successfully reduces spatial interference, these models suffer from epistemological flaws because they remain confined to stationary condition assumptions. In fact, recent theoretical kinetic studies assert that plasmas fundamentally operate in a highly transient manner, where massive cooling rates and high radiative opacity ($\tau$) force the electron density threshold to jump far beyond stationary predictions \cite{bultel_mcwhirter_2025}. This condition exposes an absolute methodological gap. Computational architectures that solely rely on spatial resolution without the intervention of transient kinetic solutions carry a strong risk of providing a false validation on spectra that are inherently non-LTE.

Addressing this methodological gap while responding to the trilemma between analytical equilibrium failure, deterministic computational paralysis, and machine learning causality fallacy, this study formulates a Physics-Informed Surrogate Model architecture resolution. This architecture fundamentally rejects the use of predictive algorithms as blind pattern matchers, but rather formulates them to operate exclusively within a pre-constrained thermodynamic manifold. To achieve this, the computational phase is split into two absolute domains. An offline forward model generator that weaves the Two-Zone partial-LTE (pLTE) thermodynamics \cite{zaitsev_two-zone_2024} with the analytical solution of the Radiative Transfer Equation (RTE), and an online Support Vector Regression (SVR) proxy \cite{wu_novel_2025}. Through this deterministic algorithmic separation, this study aims to accurately extract plasma temperature and density parameters from emission spectra distorted by self-absorption, without the need to run a single numerical RTE iteration online. In this hybrid integration, the pLTE paradigm serves to stabilize the internal excitation population from equilibrium failure, while the SVR execution guarantees that the diagnostics are executed in real-time with an absolute level of compliance to the fundamental laws of quantum mechanics. A formulation explicitly designed to conquer physical anomalies at the optically thick limit while eliminating computational time barriers in analytical spectroscopy.


\section{Methodology}

The methodological design in this study was developed using a dual-phase hybrid architecture. Unlike the conventional analytical approaches that comprehensively model the entire material components (brute-force), this computational framework is focused on modeling the main diagnostic lines. Synthetic data generation (offline forward modeling) was performed using fundamental physics modeling encompassing the dimensions of radiative transfer and pLTE equilibrium (Sections \ref{sec:rte} -- \ref{sec:aparatus}). The accumulated dataset was then utilized to train the prediction of a statistical-based interpolation model (online surrogate modeling) (Section \ref{sec:surrogate}). This two-phase approach is systematically designed to overcome the sluggishness of the iterative numerical resolution of the Radiative Transfer Equation (RTE) matrix, while simultaneously reducing empirical dependence on the stochastic black-box extraction of machine learning algorithms.

\subsection{Two-Zone Radiative Transfer Architecture} \label{sec:rte}

To capture spatial trends in a heterogeneous plasma without adding heavy computational costs at the full matrix resolution level of 3D hydrodynamic resolution, the macroscopic emission is described through the assumption of a Two-Zone distribution model comprising a dominant emission core area ($V_1$) and a peripheral marginal absorption area (Shell, $V_p$) \cite{zaitsev_two-zone_2024,hansen_modeling_2021}. Radiation transport along this non-isothermal plasma follows the fundamental Radiative Transfer Equation comprehensively in a differential manner.
\begin{equation} \label{eq:rte_general}
\frac{dI_\lambda}{ds} = j_\lambda - \kappa_\lambda I_\lambda
\end{equation}

The internal zone radiation is projected as a pure primary radiation intensity $I_{\text{core}}(\lambda)$, which is subsequently reduced and passes through the shell's local absorptional parameters (optical thickness parameter $d_{\text{shell}}$ and local constants $j_{\text{shell}}$ and $\kappa_{\text{shell}}$). When the linear integral is evaluated along the optical boundary limits.
\begin{equation} \label{eq:rte_integral}
\int_{I_{\text{core}}}^{I_{\text{obs}}} \frac{dI_\lambda}{j_{\text{shell}} - \kappa_{\text{shell}} I_\lambda} = \int_{0}^{d_{\text{shell}}} ds
\end{equation}

The representation of the final distorted spectrum observation from the plasma ($I_{\text{obs}}$) referring to this analytical transfer analysis is presented precisely moving away from the fictional approximation of Black Body thermal wave radiation \cite{espinosa_analysis_2019}.
\begin{equation} \label{eq:rte_solution}
I_{\text{obs}}(\lambda) = I_{\text{core}}(\lambda) e^{-\tau_\lambda} + \frac{j_{\text{shell}}(\lambda)}{\kappa_{\text{shell}}(\lambda)} \left( 1 - e^{-\tau_\lambda} \right)
\end{equation}
The plasma optical dimension $\tau_\lambda$ is represented by the function $\kappa_{\text{shell}} d_{\text{shell}}$, with the ratio $\frac{j}{\kappa}$ constituting the equilibrium absorption level parameter that does not purely depart from the emission-absorption identity of the asymptotic Kirchhoff model \cite{rodriguez_mixkiprapcal_2021}.

\subsection{Partial-LTE (pLTE) Kinetics Approach}

The complete conventional kinetic method tends to track transient atomic electron displacement dynamics referring to the Collisional-Radiative (CR) model matrix \cite{fujimoto_plasma_2004}.
\begin{equation} \label{eq:master_kinetic}
\frac{dn_i}{dt} = \sum_{j > i} \left( n_j A_{ji} + n_j n_e q_{ji}^{\text{down}} \right) + \sum_{j < i} n_j n_e q_{ji}^{\text{up}} - n_i \sum_{j < i} \left( A_{ij} + n_e q_{ij}^{\text{down}} \right) - n_i \sum_{j > i} n_e q_{ij}^{\text{up}}
\end{equation}
The emissivity probability variable is defined via the Einstein coefficient $A_{ji}$, alongside the components of density and collisional excitation ($n_e, q^{\text{up}}, q^{\text{down}}$). However, computational literature concerning secondary excitation level model transitions experiences uncertainty deviations (Atomic Data Desert) over the empirical atomic collisional data mechanics parameters of NIST on billions of atomic lines \cite{bultel_mcwhirter_2025,favre_merlin_2025}. The limitation of this data causes the matrix $\mathbf{M}$ in the linear rate equation system $\frac{d\mathbf{n}}{dt} = \mathbf{M} \cdot \mathbf{n}$ to become too sparse, thereby generating defective analytical resolution of the population distribution computation. Although Fujimoto has comprehensively formulated the excitation phase level of the CR matrix \cite{fujimoto_plasma_2004}, the absence of such complete empirical collisional data forces us to absolutely reduce this computational system back towards the pLTE limit.

As a transient experimental spectrum kinetic diagnostic solution, the computational structure is purely restricted and simplified into a Partial-LTE (pLTE) thermal structure. At the extreme density pressures of early LIBS transient collisions, the electron-ionic ratio equilibrium of the bound-free species is derived in a calibrated manner via the Saha Equation.
\begin{equation} \label{eq:saha}
\frac{n_{e} n_{II}}{n_{I}} = 2 \frac{Z_{II}(T_e)}{Z_{I}(T_e)} \left( \frac{2\pi m_e k_B T_e}{h^2} \right)^{3/2} \exp\left(-\frac{E_{ion}}{k_B T_e}\right)
\end{equation}
Meanwhile, the analytical probability resolution to distribute the electron configuration status of the bound excited states population is governed using a proportional exponential Boltzmann distribution approximation to compensate for the scarcity of secondary empirical collision correlations.
\begin{equation} \label{eq:boltzmann_plte}
n_i = \frac{n_{\text{total}} \cdot g_i \exp\left(-E_i / k_B T_e\right)}{Z(T_e)}
\end{equation}
The application of this pLTE enables the preservation of the physical representation of the optical absorption model spatially-macroscopically along with the independent simplification of the internal computational microscopic atomic collision cross-section level barriers \cite{cristoforetti_local_2010}.

\subsection{Assimilation of Selected Diagnostic Line Parameters}

The distributions of emission $j$ and radiative absorption $\kappa$ expressed as a macroscopic ratio (Equation \ref{eq:rte_solution}) are constructed microscopically by non-LTE thermal probability components.
\begin{equation} \label{eq:emission_coeff}
j_\lambda = \sum_{k \to i} \frac{hc}{4\pi\lambda} A_{ki} n_k \phi_V(\lambda)
\end{equation}
In addition, optical decay of radiative correlations due to photons stimulating the excitation band depletion rate (detailed balance) reinforces the magnitude of the pure absorption function convolution \cite{espinosa_analysis_2019}.
\begin{equation} \label{eq:absorption_coeff}
\kappa_\lambda = \sum_{i \to k} \frac{\lambda^4}{8\pi c} \left(\frac{g_k}{g_i}\right) A_{ki} n_i \phi_V(\lambda) \left[ 1 - \frac{n_k g_i}{n_i g_k} \right]
\end{equation}

The computational spectral modeling in this study is focused independently on the identification of experimentally validated resonance parameter lines. Comprehensive theoretical computational methods fundamentally tend to extrapolate data and agglomerate all possible secondary atomic resolution pathways polynomially \cite{favre_merlin_2025}, carrying the risk of creating vulnerable margin deviations due to unreliable simulation profiles. Hence, the simulation structure prioritizes the "Main Diagnostic Lines" (for example the resonance doublets of Ca II and Si I elements) where every foundation of its quantum mechanics emissiology can be directly referred to definitive empirical spectroscopy literature \cite{griem_spectral_1974}. The determination scheme of the element-specific resolution benchmark diagnostic aims to isolate the generative dataset (database) purely within the boundaries of the physical accuracy matrix so that the Surrogate-type algorithm in the advanced phase can be executed without the interference of stochastic correlations of minor prediction parameters \cite{tang_review_2024, manelski_libs_2024}.

\subsection{Voigt Profile Convolution and Apparatus Resolution} \label{sec:aparatus}

The distribution profile of physical thermodynamic energy dispersion on the frequency band of the linear spectroscopy function is summarized in the blended convolution curve of the mathematical function $\phi_V(\lambda)$. The composition of the convolution is calculated based on the Doppler broadening effect related to the Gaussian asymmetrical function profile of the thermal electron motion.
\begin{equation} \label{eq:broadening_doppler}
\Delta\lambda_D = \lambda_{ki} \sqrt{8 \ln 2 \frac{k_B T_i}{m_k c^2}}
\end{equation}
Along with the electrostatic interaction combination of the dominant LIBS pressure plasma turbulence resolution (Stark Broadening) that characterizes the Lorentzian profile at the electron density analyte boundary of $n_e \ge 10^{16}$ cm$^{-3}$ consistent with the empirical quantum mechanical broadening formulation by Griem \cite{griem_spectral_1974}.
\begin{equation} \label{eq:broadening_stark_simp}
\Delta\lambda_S \approx 2 \omega_{ki}(T) \frac{n_e}{n_{e,r}}
\end{equation}
The combination of the temperature density function and the Stark parameter is extracted into the Voigt convolution $\phi_V(\lambda)$. The structure of the Voigt function together with the Two-Zone RTE formula (Equation \ref{eq:rte_solution}) formulates the deformation of the spectral peak intensity due to the high optical thickness medium distortion of the plasma (self-reversal).

Furthermore, the real optical detection experiments also induce a smearing effect on the precision of the physical optical Fraunhofer absorption broadening concavity due to the interference of the instrumental micrometer spectrometer diffraction function limitations (analytical diffraction slit) \cite{zaitsev_two-zone_2024}. The final empirical form of this instrumental resolution analytical convolution modifies the simulated dispersion ($I_{obs}$) with the instrument's average Gaussian pixel distribution ($G_{inst}$) ($\Delta\lambda_{inst} \approx 0.1$ nm).
\begin{equation} \label{eq:instrumental}
I_{final}(\lambda) = I_{obs}(\lambda) \otimes G_{inst}(\lambda, \Delta\lambda_{inst})
\end{equation}
The final instrument apparatus resolution procedure bridges the theoretical parameter differences so that it is equivalently distributed in parallel matching the spectral concavity profile gradient recorded \cite{favre_merlin_2025}.

\subsection{SVR-Based Surrogate Model Training} \label{sec:surrogate}

The extractive phase of the computational algorithm of the plasma diagnostic architecture is implemented as a linear parameter resolution inversion scheme in the final stage.

Entering the pure computational (offline) phase, the simulation system generator manifests the equilibrium formulation of the RTE equation, pLTE convolution, and optical diffraction broadening (Voigt-apparatus), into a periodic system representation. The transient plasma parameters are then constructed with the iteration execution of $T_e$ starting from the value range of [4000, 20000] K against $n_e \in [10^{14}, 10^{18}]$ cm$^{-3}$ focused on validating the divalent line set of Ca II in a composite atomic mixture with Si I. This thermodynamic simulative data produces a deterministic physical curated pre-conditioned maturation domain of pLTE.

The transient emission collection is adapted to train an asymptotic Support Vector Regression (SVR) matrix specifically (online phase) \cite{wu_novel_2025, lin_enhancing_2026}. The regression learning model is deduced as a linear instant interpolation proxy (surrogate) on the inversion value of the emission identity that is actually constructed based on fundamental physics. The thermodynamic pilot approach mitigates the complexity of the iterative deterministic calculation resolution in solving the roots of the RTE inversion into an instant parametric evaluation of analytical predictive mathematical algorithm (real-time diagnostic scale). The realization of the deterministic asymptotic speed proclamation of thermodynamic SVR promises accuracy in measuring the dense line profile observational analytes regardless of the physical empirical value of the optically thick plasma laboratory.
